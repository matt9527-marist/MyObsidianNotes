**Procurement**
• Project Management Plan  
	– Helps us understand where procurement is needed (i.e. do we have the plumbing skills or do we need to hire  
a plumber). Also known as Make or Buy decision  
• Requirements documentation  
	– Describes the scope of work we are procuring  
• Activity resource requirements  
	– What skills do we need to procure  
• Enterprise environmental factors  
	– Culture and existing systems – would the seller (we hire) fit well with the organization  
• Organizational process assets  
	– Templates and standards that the organization has already in place for procurement  
• Risk register  
	– What are the risks associated with the parts we are procuring; does the procurement mitigate or create new risks  
• Stakeholder register  
	– Who are we talking to and their roles; you would not want to negotiate a contract with a non-decision maker  
• Any procurements already in place on the project  
	– How many interfaces are already in place  
• The project schedule  
	– Provides schedule constraints, required completion dates, and time estimates  
• Initial cost estimates for the work to be procured  
	– What do we think it will cost us to procure – this gives us an idea when sellers come in with their prices

• Plan Purchases and Acquisitions
	– Criteria for outside procurement
	– Make-or-buy decisions
	– Statement of Work for contract
• Plan Contracting
	– Invitation for Bids (IFBs), Request for Quotes (RFQs), Request for
Proposals (RFPs), Tender Notices, etc.
	– Evaluation criteria
• Request Supplier (Seller) Responses
	– Advertising, Bidder Conferences
• Select Suppliers
	– Price
	– Single/multiple source
	– Added value
	– Contract T’s & C’s (Terms and Conditions)
• Will guide contractor’s management processes
• Administer Contracts
	– T’s & C’s
	– Change Requests

**Management Suppliers**
• A supplier provides a product and service  
• Suppliers may be:  
	– Within the client / sponsor organization  
	– A third party  
• Types:  
	– A Supplier (or Vendor) provides a commercial off-the-shelf  
	product  
	– A Subcontractor works for hire  
	– A Contractor provides a procured skill  
• Why do we use Suppliers?  
	– Additional Resources  
	– Schedule  
	– Special Expertise  
	– Transferring Risk

**Problems that Can Occur Using Suppliers**
• Improper qualification or selection  
	– Selecting on price alone  
	– Failing to understand our needs vs. their capabilities  
• More suppliers = more risk?  
	– Increases communication paths  
• Inadequate or vague supplier RFP  
• No contract terms and conditions for damages  
• Failure to manage supplier and the deliverables  
• Supplier too close to customer  
• Relationship too informal with supplier  
• Supplier uses a subcontractor  
	– Note: you can only work with the supplier under your contract. You cannot work  
	directly with the subcontractor; only the supplier can communicate with the  
	subcontractor.  

**Role of the Project Manager**
• Coordinate make-or-buy decisions  
• Provide communication between technical & business stakeholders  
• Help prepare contract documents, including SOWs and RFPs  
• Review supplier proposals  
• Ensure funding approval and allocation  
• Act as the primary interface to the supplier  
• Responsible for supplier performance  
• Understand the supplier’s risks and how they affect the overall  
project  
• Conduct status review and reporting  
• Approve suppliers’ invoices for payment

### Ethics
**Values and Ethical Behavior**
Values: Enduring beliefs that a specific mode of conduct is preferred  
Ethical Behavior: Acting in ways consistent with values  
	 Personal values  
	 Group values  
	 Organizational values  
	 Societal values  
Ethics Management  
	 The best way to manage ethical dilemmas, like negative project risks, is to  
	avoid their occurrence in the first place.  
	 Make ethics decisions in teams, and make decisions public, as appropriate.  
	 Value forgiveness. Help project personnel recognize and address their  
mistakes and then support them to continue to try to operate ethically.  

Project Manager  
	 Stakeholder Accountability  
	 Professional Accountability  
	 Personal Accountability

**Professional and Social Responsibility**
• Ownership of Decisions and Actions  
	– Make decisions based on the best interest of the company and team, as well as society, rather than your own best  
	interest  
	– Only accept assignments you are qualified to complete  
	– If you are managing something beyond your qualifications then ensure the sponsors knows any gaps before  
	accepting the assignment  
	– Do what you say you will do  
	– Acknowledge your own errors  
	– Respect confidentiality requirements and protect proprietary information  
	– Uphold laws  
	– If you are aware of unethical behavior report it to management  
• Appropriate treatment of people and resources  
	– Maintain an attitude of mutual cooperation  
	– Respect cultural differences  
	– Do not gossip or say things that could damage another person’s reputation  
	– Engage in good faith negotiations  
	– Respect others  
	– Be direct in dealing with conflict  
	– Do not use your power or position to influence others for your own benefit

**Professional and Social Responsibility**
• Being Objective and Making Impartial Decisions  
	– Act impartially without favoritism, nepotism, bribery or prejudice, and self  
	examine yourself  
	– Look for conflicts of interest and disclose them  
	– Do not discriminate against others  
	– Honor duty of loyalty to those companies and organizations with who you  
	are affiliated  
	– Do not use your position for personal or business gain  
• Understand the Trust and take action based on truth  
	– Try to understand the truth  
	– Be truthful in all communications  
	– Work to create an environment where others tell the truth  
	– Do not deceive others
